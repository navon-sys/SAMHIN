Common Questions
Mental Health FAQs
Culturally informed answers to common mental health questions in South Asian communities.
What is the difference between a support group and therapy? ‚ñæ
A support group is a peer-facilitated space where people with shared experiences come together for mutual support ‚Äî it is not a substitute for professional mental health treatment. Therapy (or counseling) involves working one-on-one (or in small groups) with a licensed mental health professional to address specific mental health concerns. Both can be valuable, and many people benefit from both simultaneously.
How do I talk to my parents about seeking mental health help? ‚ñæ
This is one of the most common challenges in South Asian communities. Consider starting the conversation by framing mental health care similarly to physical health care ‚Äî you wouldn't ignore a broken bone, and you shouldn't ignore emotional pain either. Acknowledge the stigma exists while gently challenging it. Many families respond better when concerns are framed around performance, stress, or "being more productive" rather than "mental illness."
Are mental health services confidential? ‚ñæ
Yes. Licensed mental health professionals are legally bound by confidentiality laws. They cannot share your information without your written consent, with very limited exceptions (such as imminent risk of harm to yourself or others). SAMHIN's support groups also maintain confidentiality within the group.
What if I can't afford therapy? ‚ñæ
There are several options: (1) SAMHIN's free support groups, (2) Community mental health centers which offer sliding-scale fees, (3) University training clinics, (4) Open Path Collective for reduced-cost therapy, (5) Many insurers are required to cover mental health at the same level as physical health. Contact SAMHIN at 732-902-2561 and we can help connect you to affordable care.
Is depression the same as being sad? ‚ñæ
No. While sadness is a normal emotion that everyone experiences, clinical depression (Major Depressive Disorder) is a medical condition characterized by persistent low mood, loss of interest in activities, changes in sleep and appetite, difficulty concentrating, and sometimes thoughts of death or suicide ‚Äî lasting more than two weeks and significantly impacting daily functioning. In South Asian communities, depression may sometimes manifest more as physical symptoms (fatigue, headaches, body pain) rather than sadness.
